# Revised Chapter Structure

## Chapter 6 - Discussion

### 6.1 What Was Built
- Connect the chapter back to the introduction.
- Restate the thesis goal in plain terms.
- Summarize what the system actually delivers.
- Make the modular architecture part of the result, not the only result.

### 6.2 What It Means
- Explain why the system matters.
- Explain the use cases as evidence, not as decoration.
- Keep pick-and-place as a flexibility result.
- Keep welding as the clearest practical fit.
- Make the value proposition about reduced programming friction for repeated, frequently re-taught tasks.

### 6.3 Where It Stops
- Rank limitations by importance.
- Put scene understanding first.
- Put robot-side execution and tool abstraction next.
- Put planning, collision avoidance, and simulation next.
- Put UI and other engineering debt after that.
- Keep the evaluation boundary explicit.

### 6.4 What Not To Claim
- No universal superiority.
- No production-readiness.
- No exact performance claims.
- No claim that the linear command model is a defect.

## Chapter 7 - Conclusion And Future Work

### 7.1 Conclusion
- Short.
- Restate contribution hierarchy.
- Summarize the main takeaway modestly.

### 7.2 Near-Term Future Work
- Sensing and calibration improvements.
- Scene understanding improvements.
- Tool abstraction.
- Motion validation and collision avoidance.
- Robot-side export / compilation possibilities.
- UI and orchestration cleanup.
- Cross-platform completion where needed.

### 7.3 Longer-Term Research Directions
- Hand tracking.
- VR / AR controllers.
- Voice plus deictic gesture.
- Teach-pendant or controller integration.
- Formal user study.
- Precision characterization.
- More complex use cases.
