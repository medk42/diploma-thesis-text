\chapter{Conclusion and Future Work} \label{chapter:ch7_conclusion}

This work asked whether collaborative-robot tasks that change frequently, represented here by pick-and-place and seam welding, can be authored more directly by specifying spatial intent in the real workspace instead of relying only on lower-level programming workflows such as teach pendant programming, offline programming, or hand-guiding. The implemented system provides a positive answer for prepared task-oriented workflows.

The main result is that after a one-time setup, calibration, and integration effort, new task instances across both demonstrated task families can be authored through direct spatial input and confirmation inside a use case instead of being rebuilt as low-level robot procedures.

The rest of the chapter therefore focuses on the next steps that would broaden validation and extend the system toward a wider range of practical conditions.

\section{Near-Term Future Work}

Improving sensing, tracking, and calibration is therefore the most direct near-term step for transferring the present result beyond the demonstrated setup. More robust pen tracking, use of pen-side IMU data, and an object-detection pipeline beyond the current fiducial-box representation would directly improve the present workflow. In particular, broader applicability would benefit from a more accurate and more generally usable way to detect task-relevant objects in the scene, ideally without relying only on predefined tagged objects. These are extension directions beyond the stated goals rather than missing core deliverables of the present work, and they should be accompanied by clearer evaluation of tracking stability and scene-detection accuracy in the real workspace.

Execution semantics and safety come next. Future work should replace the current fixed-delay approximations of gripper and welding actions with an explicit tool-action abstraction. On top of that, broader deployment-oriented extensions such as motion planning, collision checking, and simulated execution can be added to the current workflow before wider real-robot use. These are natural next steps for broader deployment, not capabilities that the system set out to implement.

Applicability can be evaluated more systematically through further study. Broader physical testing and bounded quantitative characterization of tracking stability, scene-estimation repeatability, and execution consistency would make the current evidence easier to interpret. A formal comparison against teach pendant or offline workflows, ideally through a user study focused on frequently changing prepared tasks, would then help determine where this style of spatial authoring is actually advantageous and where conventional workflows remain more practical.

\section{Longer-Term Research Directions}

As the architecture is not tied to the current pen-based interface, richer input modalities remain a natural research direction. Hand tracking, VR or AR controllers, and combinations of speech and gesture could be explored within the same task-oriented model. This would help clarify which forms of spatial input remain natural for different task families while preserving the task-oriented constraints of the current approach.

A longer-term research direction is to study where this style of task-oriented spatial authoring fits best in practice. The implemented system points to promising conditions, especially tasks that are re-authored often enough that simpler spatial authoring may matter, but it does not study that practical fit systematically. Future work could examine how spatial authoring should relate to teach pendant or controller workflows and evaluate that question through a formal user study. That would help determine for which task families this approach actually reduces reprogramming effort, where prepared use cases are a good fit, and where more general robot programming remains the better tool.

That research could then guide the next stage of system development and evaluation across a broader range of task families and operating conditions. Richer welding scenarios, additional fabrication or surface-processing tasks, and other human-in-the-loop workflows in which the human provides task intent and the robot performs repeated physical execution would provide a stronger basis for judging that applicability boundary. The present result provides a concrete foundation for that work, while broader claims about applicability across task families would require corresponding extensions and evaluation on those concrete task families.
